UMN K100 Run67 - Physical Impact Ionization Model and Rates

Matt Fritts, 2 July 2018

\( \)

This note is a further attempt to model the time-dependent impact ionization seen in Run 67. We attempt to derive rates similar to Note 2 informed by Nick's model from Note 3 .

We start with the processes that Nick's model assumes are dominant:

  1. \( e^- + D^0 \rightarrow D^- \)
  2. \( h^+ + D^- \rightarrow h^+ + e^- + D^- \)

Add to this ionization from ionizing radiation. Normally I would have one ionization producing an electron/hole pair, but as will be seen it will be useful to separate into two processes, one producing holes and one producing electrons:

  1. \( I_+ \rightarrow h^+ \)
  2. \( I_- \rightarrow e^- \)

Now write the rates of electron production, hole production, and the production of charged donor states in terms of the rates of the processes above. (We assume the population of neutral donor states is constant).

  1. \( R_h = R_+ \)
  2. \( R_e = R_- - R_{eD} + R_{hD} \)
  3. \( \dot{N}_{D-} = R_{eD} - R_{hD} \)

The rates of interactions with donor states can be written as follows:

  1. \( R_{eD} = kR_e \)
  2. \( R_{hD} = pN{D-}R_h \)

where \(k\) is the probability that an electron is captured by a neutral donor and \(p\) is the probability per target that a hole impact ionizes a negatively charged donor.

Combining these yields

  1. \( \dot{N}_{D-} = \frac{k}{1+k}R_- - \frac{p}{1+k}R_+N_{D-} \)

If we assume that the population of negative donors is initially zero, we find

  1. \( N_{D-} = \frac{k}{p}\frac{R_-}{R_+}(1 - e^{-\frac{p}{1+k}R_+t}) \)
  2. \( \dot{N}_{D-} = \frac{k}{1+k}R_-e^{-\frac{p}{1+k}R_+t} \)

How well does this model fit the observations?

  • As pointed out by Nick, this model correctly predicts that only holes produce the impact ionization effect, as evidenced by the time-dependent behavior only being present in data with positive bias.
  • The model predicts that the rate of impact ionization will grow and settle exponentially to a saturation level. We observe initial linear growth; perhaps we don't see the phenomenon long enough to see the relaxation.
  • In Note 2 I tried to show how a total increase in ionization rate of 1.14 (with the Ba source present) could produce an increase in the growth rate of the impact ionization of 2.4. In this model the growth rate should be proportional to the ionization rate, but specifically to the rate of production of electrons. Under positive bias, events from the four Am sources will contribute only to the hole flux through the detector; electrons are immediately collected and don't flow through the bulk of the crystal. If the rate from Ba is 1.4X the rate from background, and if the rate from the four Am sources is 9X the rate from background, this would match the observed ratios.
